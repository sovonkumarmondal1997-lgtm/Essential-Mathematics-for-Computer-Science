# Exponential and Logarithmic Functions

---

# 1. Exponential Function

## Definition

An **Exponential Function** is a function where the variable appears in the exponent.

General form:

f(x) = b^x

where:

- b > 0
- b ≠ 1
- x is the exponent

---

## Simple Idea

Instead of multiplying x by a number:

f(x) = 2x

we raise a number to the power x:

f(x) = 2^x

This creates very fast growth or decay.

---

## Example 1

f(x)=2^x

| x | f(x) |
|---|---|
| 0 | 1 |
| 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 8 |
| 4 | 16 |

---

## Example 2

f(x)=3^x

| x | f(x) |
|---|---|
| 0 | 1 |
| 1 | 3 |
| 2 | 9 |
| 3 | 27 |

---

## Real-Life Example

Money in a bank account with compound interest often grows exponentially.

---

# 2. Exponential Base

## Definition

The **base** is the fixed number that gets multiplied repeatedly.

In:

f(x)=2^x

the base is:

2

---

## Example 1

2^4

= 2 × 2 × 2 × 2

= 16

Base = 2

---

## Example 2

5^3

= 5 × 5 × 5

= 125

Base = 5

---

## Example 3

(1/2)^3

= 1/8

Base = 1/2

---

## Real-Life Example

If bacteria doubles every hour:

Population = 2^x

Base = 2

---

# 3. Exponential Growth

## Definition

Exponential growth occurs when:

b > 1

The function increases rapidly as x increases.

---

## Example 1

f(x)=2^x

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 0 | 1 |
| 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 8 |
| 4 | 16 |

Notice growth becomes faster.

---

## Example 2

f(x)=3^x

1, 3, 9, 27, 81 ...

Rapid increase.

---

## Real-Life Examples

- Population growth
- Compound interest
- Viral videos
- Social media shares

---

# 4. Exponential Decay

## Definition

Exponential decay occurs when:

0 < b < 1

The function decreases as x increases.

---

## Example 1

f(x)=(1/2)^x

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 0 | 1 |
| 1 | 0.5 |
| 2 | 0.25 |
| 3 | 0.125 |

---

## Example 2

f(x)=(1/3)^x

1, 0.333, 0.111, 0.037 ...

---

## Real-Life Examples

- Radioactive decay
- Battery discharge
- Drug concentration reduction
- Asset depreciation

---

# 5. Domain and Range of Exponential Functions

## Domain

All real numbers.

Domain:

ℝ

---

## Why?

You can calculate:

2^5

2^0

2^-3

2^1.5

All are valid.

---

## Range

Only positive numbers.

Range:

ℝ⁺

---

## Why?

No matter what x is:

2^x > 0

Always positive.

---

## Example

f(x)=2^x

Outputs:

0.125

0.5

1

2

8

16

Never:

0

Never:

Negative values

# 6. Y-Intercept of Exponential Functions

## Definition

Every basic exponential function passes through:

(0,1)

---

## Why?

Because:

b^0=1

for every valid base.

---

## Example 1

2^0=1

Point:

(0,1)

---

## Example 2

5^0=1

Point:

(0,1)

---

## Example 3

10^0=1

Point:

(0,1)

---

## Important Fact

Every basic exponential graph crosses the y-axis at:

(0,1)

# 7. Logarithmic Function

## Definition

A logarithm asks:

"To what power must the base be raised to get a particular number?"

Notation:

log_b(x)=y

means:

b^y=x

---

## Example 1

log₂(8)=3

because:

2^3=8

---

## Example 2

log₁₀(100)=2

because:

10²=100

---

## Example 3

log₃(81)=4

because:

3⁴=81

---

## Simple Question

Exponential:

2³=8

Logarithmic:

log₂(8)=3

Same information.

---

# 8. Inverse Functional Relationship

## Definition

Logarithmic functions are the inverse of exponential functions.

---

## Example

Exponential:

f(x)=2^x

Inverse:

f⁻¹(x)=log₂(x)

---

## Example

2^3=8

Inverse relationship:

log₂(8)=3

---

## Visual Idea

Exponential:

Input → Output

3 → 8

Logarithmic:

Output → Input

8 → 3

---

# 9. Logarithmic-to-Exponential Form Conversion

## Rule

log_b(x)=y

⇔

b^y=x

---

## Example 1

log₂(16)=4

Convert:

2⁴=16

---

## Example 2

log₅(125)=3

Convert:

5³=125

---

## Example 3

log₁₀(1000)=3

Convert:

10³=1000

---

# 10. Laws of Logarithms

---

## Product Rule

log(mn)

=

log(m)+log(n)

---

### Example

log(100)

=

log(10×10)

=

log(10)+log(10)

=

1+1

=

2

---

## Quotient Rule

log(m/n)

=

log(m)-log(n)

---

### Example

log(100/10)

=

log(100)-log(10)

=

2-1

=

1

---

## Power Rule

log(mⁿ)

=

n log(m)

---

### Example

log(10³)

=

3log(10)

=

3×1

=

3

---

# 14. Reflection Symmetry Across y = x

## Definition

A function and its inverse are mirror images across:

y=x

---

## Example

Exponential:

2^3=8

Point:

(3,8)

---

## Logarithmic:

log₂(8)=3

Point:

(8,3)

---

Notice:

(3,8)

became

(8,3)

Coordinates swap.

---

## Another Example

Exponential Point:

(2,4)

Inverse Point:

(4,2)

Mirror image.

# Key Facts to Remember

1. Exponential functions have the variable in the exponent.

2. If b > 1, the graph grows.

3. If 0 < b < 1, the graph decays.

4. Exponential domain = ℝ.

5. Exponential range = ℝ⁺.

6. Logarithms are inverse exponential functions.

7. log_b(x)=y means b^y=x.

8. Logarithmic domain = ℝ⁺.

9. Logarithmic range = ℝ.

10. Exponential and logarithmic graphs are reflections across y=x.

11. ln(x)=log_e(x).

12. Euler's number is approximately 2.71828.

13. Exponential and logarithmic functions are fundamental in calculus, statistics, machine learning, artificial intelligence, finance, and data engineering.
