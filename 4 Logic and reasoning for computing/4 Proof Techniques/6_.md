# Mathematical Induction Applications - Beginner Friendly Notes

# 1. Sum of Geometric Powers of Two

## Statement

\[ 2^0+2^1+2^2+`\cdots`{=tex}+2^{n-1}=2\^n-1 \]

This says that if you keep adding powers of 2, the answer is always
**one less than the next power of 2**.

## Example

For n = 4

Left side:

1 + 2 + 4 + 8 = 15

Right side:

2⁴ - 1 = 16 - 1 = 15

They match.

## Induction Idea

### Base Case

n = 1

Left:

2⁰ = 1

Right:

2¹ - 1 = 1

True.

### Inductive Hypothesis

Assume

2⁰ + 2¹ + ... + 2\^(k-1) = 2\^k - 1

### Inductive Step

Add the next term 2\^k.

Left side becomes

(2\^k - 1) + 2\^k

= 2·2\^k - 1

= 2\^(k+1) - 1

So the formula also works for k+1.

Therefore it is true for all natural numbers.

# 2. Algebraic Substitution via Hypothesis

## What is it?

This is the most important step in an induction proof.

Instead of expanding a long expression again, replace the first k terms
with the simpler formula that you already assumed to be true.

Think of it as using a shortcut.

## Example

Suppose the hypothesis is

1+2+...+k = k(k+1)/2

To prove the next case:

1+2+...+k+(k+1)

replace the highlighted part:

k(k+1)/2 + (k+1)

Now the problem becomes much easier.

## Another Example

Hypothesis:

2⁰+2¹+...+2^(k-1)=2^k-1

Next step:

2⁰+...+2^(k-1)+2^k

Replace the first k terms:

(2^k-1)+2^k

Then simplify.

# 3. Inequality Induction (Growth Proof)

## Statement

For every natural number,

n \< 3\^n

This means the exponential function 3\^n eventually grows much faster
than the linear function n.

## Small Examples

n=1

1 \< 3 ✓

n=2

2 \< 9 ✓

n=5

5 \< 243 ✓

As n becomes larger, the gap grows rapidly.

## Induction Proof

### Base Case

n=1

1\<3

True.

### Inductive Hypothesis

Assume

k \< 3\^k

### Inductive Step

Need to prove

k+1 \< 3\^(k+1)

Start with

k+1

Use the fact that

1 ≤ 3\^k

(for natural numbers k≥0)

So

k+1 \< 3\^k + 3\^k

= 2·3\^k

Since

2·3\^k \< 3·3\^k

= 3\^(k+1)

Therefore

k+1 \< 3\^(k+1)

Hence the statement is true for all natural numbers.

# 4. Inequality Dominance Manipulation

## What is it?

Sometimes an induction proof contains a small number like 1.

Instead of keeping it, replace it with something **larger** that you
already know.

This makes the expression easier to factor.

This trick is called dominance manipulation.

## Example

We know from the hypothesis that

3\^k ≥ 1

Replace

1

with

3\^k

Then

k+1

\< k + 3\^k

Using the hypothesis

k \< 3\^k

gives

k+1

\< 3\^k + 3\^k

= 2·3\^k

\< 3·3\^k

= 3\^(k+1)

The larger expression is easier to simplify and still preserves the
inequality.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Real-Life Intuition

Linear growth:

1,2,3,4,5,...

Exponential growth:

3,9,27,81,243,...

At first they seem close, but exponential growth quickly becomes much
larger.

Examples:

-   Compound interest
-   Population growth
-   Computing power
-   Search-tree sizes in algorithms
