# Proof Techniques - Beginner Friendly Notes

# 1. What is a Proof?

A **proof** is a logical explanation that shows why a mathematical
statement is always true.

A proof does not rely on guessing or a few examples. It uses
definitions, facts, and logical reasoning.

Think of it like solving a mystery by showing every clue step by step.

## Example 1

Claim: The sum of two even numbers is even.

Proof idea:

Even numbers can be written as 2a and 2b.

2a + 2b = 2(a+b)

Since (a+b) is an integer, the result is divisible by 2.

Therefore, the sum is even.

## Example 2

Claim: 5 + 3 = 8

Instead of just believing it, we can prove it using the rules of
arithmetic.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 2. Why Do We Need Proof?

Proofs help us know that something is **always true**, not just true in
a few cases.

Why proofs matter:

-   Verify mathematical statements.
-   Avoid mistakes.
-   Build reliable algorithms.
-   Design correct software and hardware.
-   Create trustworthy scientific results.

## Example

Checking:

2² = 4

3² = 9

4² = 16

does NOT prove that "every square is non-negative."

A proof does.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 3. Direct Proof

A direct proof starts from known facts, definitions, or assumptions and
moves step by step until the conclusion is reached.

General pattern:

Assumption → Logical steps → Conclusion

## Example

Claim:

If n is even, then n² is even.

Proof:

Assume n = 2k.

n² = (2k)² = 4k² = 2(2k²)

Therefore n² is even.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 4. Definition-Based Starting Step

Before proving anything, clearly state the definition.

## Example

To prove:

"The sum of two even numbers is even."

Start with the definition:

An even integer has the form:

2k

where k is an integer.

Only then continue the proof.

Another example:

Odd number = 2k + 1.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 5. Closure under Addition

Closure means an operation keeps you inside the same set.

For integers:

Integer + Integer = Integer

## Examples

5 + 8 = 13

-4 + 10 = 6

0 + 7 = 7

All results are still integers.

Counterexample:

Positive integers are NOT closed under subtraction.

3 - 5 = -2

Not positive.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 6. Proof by Case Analysis

Sometimes one proof is divided into several possible cases.

If every case works, the statement is proved.

## Example 1

Claim:

Every integer is either even or odd.

Case 1:

n is even.

Case 2:

n is odd.

Since every integer belongs to one case, the proof is complete.

## Example 2

Numbers divided by 3 leave one of three remainders:

3k

3k+1

3k+2

To prove a statement about all integers, check all three cases.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 7. Inequality Sign Flips

Rule:

When multiplying or dividing both sides of an inequality by a negative
number, reverse the inequality sign.

## Examples

3 \> 2

Multiply by -1

-3 \< -2

Notice \> became \<.

Another example:

x \> 5

Multiply by -2

-2x \< -10

The sign flips.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 8. Commutative Property of Multiplication

Changing the order of multiplication does not change the answer.

Formula:

a × b = b × a

## Examples

3 × 5 = 5 × 3 = 15

7 × 2 = 2 × 7 = 14

Algebra:

xy = yx

This property is often used to rearrange terms during proofs.
