# Free Fall Motion: Initial Velocity, Gravity, Final Velocity, Distance, and Units

---

# Introduction

One of the simplest and most important applications of the SUVAT equations is:

```text
Free Fall Motion
```

Free fall occurs when an object moves under the influence of gravity alone.

Examples include:

- Dropping a stone from a cliff
- Dropping a phone from a balcony
- Releasing a ball from your hand
- A skydiver before opening a parachute

In these situations, gravity provides a constant acceleration, making the SUVAT equations perfect tools for solving the motion.

---

# Example Scenario

Suppose a stone is dropped from a cliff.

Given:

```text
Initial velocity (u) = 0 m/s
Acceleration (a) = 10 m/s²
Time (t) = 4 s
```

We want to determine:

1. Final velocity
2. Distance fallen

---

# 1. Initial Velocity from Rest (u = 0)

## What Does "Dropped from Rest" Mean?

When a question says:

```text
The object is dropped
```

it means:

```text
No push was given.
```

The object simply starts falling because of gravity.

---

# Initial Velocity

The starting velocity is called:

\[
u
\]

---

# If an Object is Dropped

Then:

\[
u = 0
\]

because the object begins completely stationary.

---

# Example 1

Drop a stone from a building.

At the exact moment you release it:

```text
Velocity = 0 m/s
```

Therefore:

\[
u=0
\]

---

# Example 2

Drop a coin from your hand.

Before release:

```text
Coin is not moving.
```

Thus:

\[
u=0
\]

---

# Example 3

Drop a book from a desk.

Initially:

```text
Speed = 0
```

Therefore:

\[
u=0
\]

---

# Important Difference

### Dropped

```text
u = 0
```

---

### Thrown Downward

Example:

```text
Thrown downward at 5 m/s
```

Then:

\[
u=5m/s
\]

---

### Thrown Upward

Example:

```text
Thrown upward at 20 m/s
```

Then:

\[
u=20m/s
\]

---

# Summary

| Situation | Initial Velocity |
|------------|------------|
| Dropped | 0 m/s |
| Thrown Up | Positive value |
| Thrown Down | Positive or negative depending on sign convention |

---

# Key Idea

When you see:

```text
Dropped from rest
```

immediately write:

\[
u=0
\]

---

# 2. Using Gravity (g) as Constant Acceleration

## What is Gravity?

Gravity is the force that pulls objects toward Earth.

---

# Gravitational Acceleration

Near Earth's surface:

\[
g=9.8m/s^2
\]

---

# Simplified Value

In many beginner problems:

\[
g=10m/s^2
\]

is used for easier calculations.

---

# What Does 10 m/s² Mean?

It means:

```text
Velocity increases by 10 m/s every second.
```

---

# Example

Suppose a stone is dropped.

| Time (s) | Velocity (m/s) |
|-----------|-----------|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 10 |
| 2 | 20 |
| 3 | 30 |
| 4 | 40 |

Every second:

```text
Velocity increases by 10 m/s.
```

---

# Why Is Acceleration Constant?

Near Earth's surface:

```text
Gravity pulls almost equally at all heights.
```

Therefore:

\[
a=g=10m/s^2
\]

can be treated as constant.

---

# Real-Life Example

Drop:

- A tennis ball
- A rock
- A phone

Ignoring air resistance, all accelerate at:

\[
10m/s^2
\]

---

# Summary

For free-fall questions:

\[
a=g
\]

Usually:

\[
a=10m/s^2
\]

---

# 3. Calculating Final Velocity

## Goal

Determine how fast the object is moving after a given time.

---

# Formula

Use SUVAT Equation 2:

\[
v=u+at
\]

---

# Meaning

Final velocity equals:

```text
Initial velocity
+
Acceleration × Time
```

---

# Example Problem

Given:

\[
u=0
\]

\[
a=10m/s^2
\]

\[
t=4s
\]

Find:

\[
v
\]

---

# Step 1: Write Formula

\[
v=u+at
\]

---

# Step 2: Substitute Values

\[
v=0+(10)(4)
\]

---

# Step 3: Calculate

\[
v=40
\]

---

# Final Answer

\[
\boxed{v=40m/s}
\]

---

# Interpretation

After:

```text
4 seconds
```

the object is moving downward at:

```text
40 meters per second.
```

---

# Velocity Growth Visualization

```text
Time = 0s → 0 m/s
Time = 1s → 10 m/s
Time = 2s → 20 m/s
Time = 3s → 30 m/s
Time = 4s → 40 m/s
```

---

# Real-Life Analogy

Think of a car accelerating steadily:

```text
Each second adds more speed.
```

Gravity does exactly the same thing to a falling object.

---

# 4. Calculating Distance Traveled (s)

## Goal

Determine how far the object falls.

---

# Formula

Use SUVAT Equation 3:

\[
s=ut+\frac12 at^2
\]

---

# Meaning

Distance equals:

```text
Distance from starting velocity
+
Distance from acceleration
```

---

# Example Problem

Given:

\[
u=0
\]

\[
a=10
\]

\[
t=4
\]

---

# Step 1: Write Formula

\[
s=ut+\frac12 at^2
\]

---

# Step 2: Substitute Values

\[
s=(0)(4)+\frac12(10)(4^2)
\]

---

# Step 3: Simplify

First term:

\[
(0)(4)=0
\]

---

Second term:

\[
\frac12(10)(16)
\]

\[
=5(16)
\]

\[
=80
\]

---

# Step 4: Add

\[
s=0+80
\]

\[
s=80
\]

---

# Final Answer

\[
\boxed{s=80m}
\]

---

# Interpretation

After:

```text
4 seconds
```

the object has fallen:

```text
80 meters.
```

---

# Why Doesn't Distance Increase Uniformly?

Notice:

| Time (s) | Distance (m) |
|-----------|-----------|
| 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 20 |
| 3 | 45 |
| 4 | 80 |

Distance grows faster and faster because velocity keeps increasing.

---

# Visual Representation

```text
0s → 0 m
1s → 5 m
2s → 20 m
3s → 45 m
4s → 80 m
```

---

# Real-Life Example

If a cliff is:

```text
80 meters high
```

then a dropped stone reaches the ground after approximately:

```text
4 seconds.
```

Numbers have no meaning.

# Final Key Idea

For free-fall motion:

```text
Dropped from rest
```

means:

\[
u=0
\]

and gravity provides a constant acceleration:

\[
a=g\approx10m/s^2
\]

Using the SUVAT equations:

\[
v=u+at
\]

and

\[
s=ut+\frac12at^2
\]

we can calculate how fast an object is moving and how far it has fallen after any given time.

Always remember to include the correct units:

- Velocity → m/s
- Distance → m
- Time → s
- Acceleration → m/s²

These principles form the foundation of projectile motion, engineering mechanics, robotics, aerospace engineering, and physics.
