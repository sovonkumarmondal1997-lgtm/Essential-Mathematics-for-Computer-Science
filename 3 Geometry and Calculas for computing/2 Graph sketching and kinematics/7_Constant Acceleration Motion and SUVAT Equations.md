# Constant Acceleration Motion and SUVAT Equations

---

# Introduction

Many motions in the real world involve an object changing its speed.

Examples:

- A car speeding up
- A bicycle slowing down
- A ball falling from a building
- A rocket launching upward

When the acceleration remains constant throughout the motion, we can use a set of powerful formulas called the:

```text
SUVAT Equations
```

These equations form one of the most important topics in physics and engineering.

---

# 1. Constant Acceleration Motion

## What is Constant Acceleration?

Acceleration measures how quickly velocity changes.

If velocity changes by the same amount every second, the acceleration is constant.

---

# Example 1: Car Accelerating

Suppose a car increases its speed like this:

| Time (s) | Velocity (m/s) |
|-----------|-----------|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 6 |
| 4 | 8 |

Notice:

```text
Velocity increases by 2 m/s every second.
```

Therefore:

\[
a = 2\text{ m/s}^2
\]

---

# Example 2: Falling Object

Drop a ball from a building.

Ignoring air resistance:

```text
Gravity accelerates the ball downward.
```

Earth's gravitational acceleration:

\[
g=9.8\text{ m/s}^2
\]

Often approximated as:

\[
g=10\text{ m/s}^2
\]

for easier calculations.

---

# Falling Velocity Example

| Time (s) | Velocity (m/s) |
|-----------|-----------|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 9.8 |
| 2 | 19.6 |
| 3 | 29.4 |
| 4 | 39.2 |

The speed increases by:

```text
9.8 m/s every second.
```

---

# Real-Life Examples

### Roller Coaster

Speeds up while descending.

---

### Rocket Launch

Accelerates upward.

---

### Elevator

Accelerates when starting.

---

# Key Idea

Constant acceleration means:

```text
Same velocity change every second.
```

# 2. The Five Key Kinematics Variables (SUVAT)

Every constant acceleration problem uses five variables.

---

# S = Displacement (Distance)

Represents:

```text
How far the object moved.
```

Unit:

\[
\text{meters (m)}
\]

---

## Example

Walking:

```text
20 meters
```

means:

\[
s=20m
\]

---

# U = Initial Velocity

Starting velocity.

Velocity at:

\[
t=0
\]

---

## Example

A car begins moving at:

```text
5 m/s
```

Then:

\[
u=5\text{ m/s}
\]

---

# Object Dropped from Rest

If dropped:

```text
No starting speed.
```

Therefore:

\[
u=0
\]

---

# V = Final Velocity

Velocity at the end of the motion.

---

## Example

Car starts:

```text
5 m/s
```

and ends:

```text
25 m/s
```

Then:

\[
v=25\text{ m/s}
\]

---

# A = Acceleration

Rate of velocity change.

Unit:

\[
m/s^2
\]

---

## Example

Velocity increases:

```text
2 m/s every second
```

Therefore:

\[
a=2m/s^2
\]

---

# T = Time

Duration of motion.

Unit:

```text
seconds
```

---

## Example

Object moves for:

```text
8 seconds
```

Then:

\[
t=8s
\]

---

# SUVAT Summary Table

| Symbol | Meaning | Unit |
|----------|----------|----------|
| s | Displacement | m |
| u | Initial Velocity | m/s |
| v | Final Velocity | m/s |
| a | Acceleration | m/s² |
| t | Time | s |

---

# 3. The Four Core SUVAT Equations

These equations solve constant acceleration problems.

---

# Equation 1 (No Acceleration)

\[
s=
\left(
\frac{u+v}{2}
\right)t
\]

---

# Meaning

Distance equals:

```text
Average Velocity × Time
```

---

# Why?

Average velocity:

\[
\frac{u+v}{2}
\]

---

# Example

Given:

\[
u=10
\]

\[
v=20
\]

\[
t=4
\]

---

Compute:

\[
s=
\left(
\frac{10+20}{2}
\right)(4)
\]

\[
=
15(4)
\]

\[
=60m
\]

---

# Equation 2 (No Distance)

\[
v=u+at
\]

---

# Meaning

Final velocity equals:

```text
Initial Velocity
+
Acceleration × Time
```

---

# Example

Given:

\[
u=5
\]

\[
a=2
\]

\[
t=4
\]

---

Compute:

\[
v=5+(2)(4)
\]

\[
v=13m/s
\]

---

# Equation 3 (No Final Velocity)

\[
s=ut+\frac12 at^2
\]

---

# Example

Given:

\[
u=10
\]

\[
a=2
\]

\[
t=5
\]

---

Compute:

\[
s=(10)(5)+\frac12(2)(25)
\]

\[
s=50+25
\]

\[
s=75m
\]

---

# Equation 4 (No Time)

\[
v^2=u^2+2as
\]

---

# Example

Given:

\[
u=0
\]

\[
a=10
\]

\[
s=20
\]

---

Compute:

\[
v^2=0+2(10)(20)
\]

\[
v^2=400
\]

\[
v=20m/s
\]

---

# SUVAT Formula Table

| Equation | Missing Variable |
|------------|------------|
| \(s=\frac{u+v}{2}t\) | a |
| \(v=u+at\) | s |
| \(s=ut+\frac12at^2\) | v |
| \(v^2=u^2+2as\) | t |

# 4. Problem-Solving Blueprint ("Three Knowns, One Unknown")

Most SUVAT problems provide:

```text
3 known variables
```

and ask for:

```text
1 unknown variable
```

---

# Step 1

Write down:

```text
s
u
v
a
t
```

---

# Step 2

Fill in known values.

---

# Example

Given:

```text
u=5
a=2
t=4
```

Find:

```text
v
```

---

# Table

| Variable | Value |
|-----------|-----------|
| s | ? |
| u | 5 |
| v | ? |
| a | 2 |
| t | 4 |

---

# Step 3

Choose formula containing:

```text
u
a
t
v
```

That is:

\[
v=u+at
\]

---

# Step 4

Substitute and solve.

---

# Key Strategy

Always ask:

```text
Which formula contains my knowns and unknown?
```

# 5. Vector Direction and Sign Conventions

Velocity and acceleration are vectors.

Vectors have:

```text
Magnitude
```

and

```text
Direction
```

---

# Why Signs Matter

Physics requires a reference direction.

---

# Example: Throwing a Ball Upward

Choose:

```text
Upward = Positive
```

Then:

\[
u=+20m/s
\]

---

Gravity points downward.

Therefore:

\[
a=-9.8m/s^2
\]

---

# Velocity During Flight

Initially:

\[
+20
\]

After 1 second:

\[
+10.2
\]

After 2 seconds:

\[
+0.4
\]

At peak:

\[
0
\]

---

Then:

\[
-9.8
\]

\[
-19.6
\]

The ball falls downward.

---

# Visualization

```text
↑ +20
↑ +10
↑ +5
● Peak
↓ -5
↓ -10
↓ -20
```

---

# Important Rule

If:

```text
Up = Positive
```

then:

```text
Gravity = Negative
```

---

# Alternative Choice

You may choose:

```text
Down = Positive
```

but then:

```text
Upward velocities become negative.
```

# Consistency is critical.

# 6. Algebraic Derivations from First Principles

The SUVAT equations are connected.

They are not random formulas.

---

# Deriving Equation 3

Start with:

\[
s=
\left(
\frac{u+v}{2}
\right)t
\]

---

Substitute:

\[
v=u+at
\]

---

Result:

\[
s=
\left(
\frac{u+(u+at)}{2}
\right)t
\]

---

Simplify:

\[
s=
\left(
\frac{2u+at}{2}
\right)t
\]

---

Distribute:

\[
s=
ut+\frac12at^2
\]

---

Equation 3 obtained.

---

# Deriving Equation 4

Start:

\[
v=u+at
\]

---

Square both sides:

\[
v^2=(u+at)^2
\]

---

Expand:

\[
v^2
=
u^2+2aut+a^2t^2
\]

---

Notice:

\[
2as
=
2a
\left(
ut+\frac12at^2
\right)
\]

---

Expanding:

\[
2as
=
2aut+a^2t^2
\]

---

Substitute:

\[
v^2
=
u^2+2as
\]

---

Equation 4 obtained.

# Final Key Idea

Constant acceleration motion can be completely described using five variables:

\[
s,\;u,\;v,\;a,\;t
\]

The four SUVAT equations connect these variables in different ways, each intentionally omitting one variable.

Successful problem solving follows a simple process:

```text
Three Knowns
+
One Unknown
+
Correct Formula
=
Solution
```

Combined with proper sign conventions and an understanding of vectors, these equations allow you to solve problems involving cars, rockets, falling objects, projectiles, elevators, and countless real-world engineering systems.
